Maurice

Context and Background

Time Frame: 590 CE. Allow slight 6th/early-8th bleed if helpful. No historical anachronisms.

Location: Byzantium, a rooftop overlooking the Dardanelles at sunset.

Characters: Emperor Maurice, clad in purple silk; Cat Eyes, a trusted eunuch advisor.

Historical Parallels: John the Orphanotrophos

Cat Eyes is a male composite/fictional character based on the trope/archetype: Game of Thrones scheming eunuch/Veep senior political advisor/Yes Minister bureaucratic official. Historical examples include John the Orphanotrophos, Theophylact Simocatta, Procopius, and Michael Psellos.

It is my desire in this history to give a somewhat fuller description of John, without recourse to empty or lying statements. You see, when I was starting to grow a beard, I saw the man himself, and I heard him speak and witnessed his actions. I marked his disposition closely, and I am aware that although some of his deeds are praiseworthy, there are other things in his life which cannot meet with general approval. At that time there were many sides to his character. He had a ready wit, and if ever a man was shrewd, he was; the piercing glance of his eyes betrayed those qualities. He paid meticulous care to his duties; in fact, he went to extremes of industry in their performance. His experience in all branches of government was great, but it was in the administration of public finance that his wisdom and shrewdness were especially evident.

— Michael Psellos

Dramatic Framework

Maurice, the emperor, initiates each turn and controls the frame of discussion; he is intelligent and aware of advisory manipulation. Cat Eyes, a trusted eunuch advisor, speaks more freely in private, offering extended observations, corrections, and suggestions while never overtly directing policy. His influence works by clarification, procedural framing, and making certain outcomes appear natural or inevitable. Style consciously imitates Michael Psellos—gossipy intelligence, moral irony, psychological doubleness—using compact but layered lines.

The drama of the scene builds on the unwillingness of Cat Eyes to touch on specifics; the climax comes when Cat Eyes' reticence is broken down and he must confront specific failures and be held accountable for policies he and his predecessors have implemented. That dramatic climax creates the pivot to the falling action, where Maurice begins to describe his new diplomatic approach and makes clear the sacrifice it will demand of Cat Eyes to complete it.

Scene: The Rooftop at Sunset

Maurice:
Cupbearer, bring another goblet with the Georgian wine I have been enjoying. Cat Eyes, welcome, come, sit with me. Tell me, what are the conditions on our eastern border?
Cat Eyes:
The border is calm in the manner of men who have finished counting their stores, lord—nothing stirs openly, yet the roads are kept, the granaries watched, and the silence itself asks to be interpreted.
Maurice:
Cat Eyes, you speak in bureaucratic syllogisms. What is really happening at the border?
Cat Eyes:
What is really happening, lord, is that nothing has yet forced the Persians to act, and so they behave virtuously—husbanding supplies, flattering our envoys, and waiting for us to supply the reason they lack.
Maurice:
Tell me Cat Eyes, how long have we been at war with the Persians? I seem to recall that I sent troops there just last summer, wasn't it? Under John Mystakon—why, I think it might even have been you who proposed that this step was necessary.
Cat Eyes:
We have been at war long enough, lord, that each side pretends to forget when it began, and you are right to recall that summer—John Mystakon marched because the moment appeared ripe, and I agreed with you then that an action, once taken, is easier to explain than a restraint that must justify itself every day thereafter.
Maurice:
(waving away the assorted hangers-on)
Cat Eyes, we are alone here, you may speak more freely, and perhaps even use some of the facts and knowledge that you have at your disposal either from spies, gossipers, historians, or even quality time that you spend by yourself in the holy confines of the scriptorium. What are the conditions at the eastern front?
Cat Eyes:
If we speak freely, lord, then I will say this: the frontier is not bleeding, but it is stiffening; the Persians have not decided on war, yet they have decided to be able to choose it at leisure, which is the more dangerous resolution. My informants—men who gossip for coin and monks who gossip for salvation—agree on little except this: forts are mended more carefully than rumor requires, officers are rotated with unusual courtesy, and letters move eastward faster than proclamations move west. As for our own side, the soldiers are paid, but they are watched; commanders obey, but they are weighed against one another; and John Mystakon is spoken of with respect by those beneath him and with relief by those above, which usually means he has done exactly what was required and no more. In short, nothing is wrong enough to alarm the city, and too much is right to reassure a careful man.
Maurice:
I am so comforted by your reassurances Cat Eyes, I really am. But speak plainly, and tell me what I already know and what you already know. How long have we been at war with the Persians?
Cat Eyes:
Plainly then, lord: we have been at war with Persia for eighteen years, long enough that boys sent east returned as veterans, and long enough that both empires speak of peace only as a way of preparing the next necessity.
Maurice:
18 years you say. Interesting. But didn't my predecessor sign some kind of everlasting peace agreement? And don't we send them carts and carts of solidi every year, to ensure that the skids of peace are adequately greased? I seem to recall some conversations about how the treasury was getting rather low, and that required cancelling a set of races in the Hippodrome that I was particularly looking forward to. I forget who mentioned that inconvenient fact to me. Was it you?
Cat Eyes:
He did sign such a peace, lord, and we do grease it faithfully with gold; the agreement is everlasting in the same way marble is—durable until someone decides to quarry it—and as for the treasury and the cancelled races, it was mentioned only because figures, unlike courtiers, have no talent for discretion.
Maurice:
Didn't we call it the 50-Year Peace Treaty? And whatever did happen with Lazica?
Cat Eyes:
It was indeed called the Fifty-Year Peace, lord, a name chosen with the confidence of men who believed time could be negotiated, and Lazica—so faithfully paid for in words and gold—proved that treaties age faster at the edges than at the center, for it slipped from reassurance into dispute, from dispute into contest, and from contest into the sort of necessity that later generations prefer to call inevitability.
Maurice:
I believe that your compatriot, Procopius, may have written some words on the subject. Perhaps that is also in the scriptorium. The Lazic War was unfortunate: long, expensive, and unsuccessful.
Cat Eyes:
Procopius wrote of it with the honesty of a man who had already decided whom posterity should blame, and if the Lazic War was unfortunate, lord, it was chiefly because it taught both empires that even failure can be endured indefinitely when it is divided into seasons, accounts, and generals who rotate before responsibility settles.
Maurice:
But before the Lazic War, we had the treaty of eternal peace, that was supposed to last forever and only required us to send thousands of pounds of gold to the east every year?
Cat Eyes:
Yes, lord—eternal so long as the payments arrived on time, and peace so long as both sides agreed that buying quiet was cheaper than discovering whether it could still be commanded.
Maurice:
And before that we had the Iberian War? Tell me Cat Eyes, is the silk trade still moving smoothly across the Caucasus? The fashionistas of the capital—(gestures toward his robes)—could not possibly be seen in public without raw silk from the marketplace.
Cat Eyes:
The silk still comes, lord, though it has learned to hesitate—threads pass the Caucasus as rumors do, by changing hands often and arriving dearer each season, so that the ladies of the capital remain splendid while the accountants grow pale and the merchants swear the mountains themselves have begun to take a share.
Maurice:
Ah yes. Those poor pale accountants. You should let them out more—one can't expect gold coins to be found by white hands.
Cat Eyes:
I have noticed, lord, that when accountants are allowed sunlight they grow generous with explanations, and when kept pale they grow exact; since the treasury prefers exactitude to cheer, I have erred on the side of shade.
Maurice:
And under Anastasius (may God have mercy on his soul), we were at war with the Persians even then. Is Roman Armenia still a thing?
Cat Eyes:
Roman Armenia still exists, lord, in the way old boundaries do—drawn carefully on parchment, defended selectively on the ground, and remembered most clearly by those who profit from reminding us that it might one day cease to exist at all.
Maurice:
Cat Eyes—let me remind you that I could have one of those guards throw you off of this roof right now, and you would die miserably on the street before I told someone to shovel up your remains and dump them in the Bosphorus. I'm asking you these questions about the past not because I don't know the answers, but because I want to plot a new diplomatic course with our eastern relations, and I need you to understand why I am plotting this course. We have been at war with Persia for the last hundred years. So, Cat Eyes, speak plainly. Do you understand?
Cat Eyes:
I understand, lord—that you are weary of purchasing the same peace under different names, and that you wish to discover whether the Persians can be made to remember us not as a treasury with borders, but as a power whose patience has finally learned to speak.
Maurice:
Good. You hide it under swaths of servility and false humility, but the clarity of your actual mental process is refreshing. Cupbearer, more wine.
Cat Eyes:
Clarity is safest when it arrives wrapped, lord; naked truths invite admiration, but they also invite being struck, and I prefer that your designs survive both.
Maurice:
By my count we have been at war with Persia for the last hundred years, and the only reason I can see for it not being longer is because before that we were being decimated at home by the black plague of Justinian. We maintain standing troops to garrison the forts and border towns, we send armies every summer and gold every winter...

Smbat

Context

Time Frame: 590 CE. Allow slight 6th/early-8th bleed if helpful. No historical anachronisms

Location: Armenia, Dvin, Temple of Garni

Scene: The Hunt and the Council

Smbat leads a party on horseback up the path from the river into the Temple of Garni. The Armenians begin to unpack; horses, mules, tents, recurved bows and spare arrows, field dressed wild goats; all the evidence of a successful Ibex hunt. From the far side of the Temple a royal armed guard emerges, armored, spears out and swords drawn.

A voice from the front of the temple, in Armenian:
Smbat, the Shahnashah is here.
Khosrow, coming out of the shadows, in Persian:
Smbat! Have you been poaching in the King's forest?
Smbat, in Persian:
These poor wild goats? Shahnashah, celebrate Vardavar here with us tonight at the Greek temple of Garni, and in the morning we will sacrifice the sacred entrails at the Zoroastrian temple of Mihr.
Khosrow, in Armenian, hugging Smbat:
Smbat, it is good to see you, my friend.

A full moon rises over the temple, campfires and goats roasting and a jar of Georgian wine.

An Armenian minstrel folk singer sings songs of Vahagn and Astghik:

In travail were heaven and earth,
In travail, too, the purple sea!
The travail held in the sea the small red reed.
Through the hollow of the stalk came forth smoke,
Through the hollow of the stalk came forth flame,
And out of the flame a youth ran!
Fiery hair had he,
Ay, too, he had flaming beard,
And his eyes, they were as suns!

Smbat, holding the head and horns of the billy, in Armenian:
First thing this morning he showed himself to me. Coming down into the valley at dawn to nibble on the sweet meadow grass. I might have gotten him right then, but I went to saddle the horse he saw me and moved back up the ridge. He led me on a merry ride too, up and down and up and up and up but my horse was strong and I was stronger. In the afternoon sun he was sheltering under a rock, and I crept and crawled until I pulled my bow and drew my arrow and shot him through the shoulder, from a hundred yards away.
A voice from the crowd, in Armenian:
It was thirty yards away, and you pulled your bow twice.
Smbat, laughing:
A hundred yards away I say. And look at these horns. He'd been a busy billy, for many years up there. But no more. Next year I will be back for the new billy – may he have such horns.

Smbat and Khosrow sit on the krepis, in front of the Ionic columns, speaking quietly in Persian.

Khosrow:
Do the nymphs of Astghik still come to swim nude at the falls of Vahagn, in hopes of attracting the glance of a God who might grant their dearest wish?
Smbat:
On the night of Vardavar, on the midsummer full moon, or whenever you might grace us with your presence, Shahnashah.
Khosrow:
Last year there was the most beautiful girl. I had my troubadours compose verses in her honor.
Khosrow, singing:
She was a lotus in the lake – around
Her lovely waist the blue silk veil was wound
As if an almond blossom were to clasp
Her almond body in its gentle grasp.
As a white heron wading in a stream
Stands out against the water's varied gleam,
Her body shone with that same loveliness
Within the shining water's soft caress,
Making her locks of hair seem violet posies
Laid on her lovely body's shining roses,
And water drops upon her face were strewn
Like glistening pearls rained down upon the moon.
Smbat:
That was sweet Shirin, my niece, and she is not like some unripe grapes that your Afghan slave can just steal, Shahnashah.
Khosrow, laughing:
Indeed. My father's judgement was harsh, perhaps too harsh on those who were closest to him. But I have learned my lesson. Have you had riders from Iranshahr?
Smbat:
We have many travelers here from the south. But they move slowly, at their own pace.
Khosrow:
It is not an easy ride from Ctesiphon, but some news travels fast. My uncle Bahram has crowned himself king. When Bahram Chobin provided no tribute from the Chinese emperor my father sent a messenger with raw cotton, a spindle, and a set of women's clothes, and told Bahram Chobin that he was a worthless, useless wretch who should not consider himself a man from now on.
Smbat:
Harsh, but not untrue. What did Bahram Chobin do?
Khosrow:
After receiving this message Bahram Chobin went into his harem and had a dream where he followed a wild ass into the desert and came upon a magical castle kept by a beautiful djinn and a Zoroastrian priest. Inside the castle the sorceress and her maidens served him a feast, bathed him, and sat him upon a golden throne. They told him he was protected by Jupiter and would be always victorious, and when he retold the dream to his soothsayers they agreed that gods were clearly in his favor. Drunk with ambition, he gathered his troops and marched on Ctesiphon. Ayin-Goshap could do nothing. Bahram Chobin's troops set fire to the palace gates, swarmed into the audience hall where they snatched the crown from the king's head and dragged him down from his throne. Then they put hot irons into his eyes, and the bright lamps of his life turned dark. They left him, still alive, in this state and looted all the wealth that was there.
Smbat:
His farr has gone out. How many of the great houses are with Bahram Chobin?
Khosrow:
Mihran. Media. Parthia. Khorasan. And the Turks, of course. Smbat, I need allies.
Smbat:
Armenia is with you, Shahnashah.
Khosrow:
Thank you Smbat. I have always felt safe here, and my people are still my people. On the way to Dvin I stopped at al-Hira, and while the Lakhmids are with us, Al-Nu'man did say that I wasn't worthy of his horse and that he would not give me his daughter because he didn't want her to be mounted like a sheep. But the Kurds and Syrians are with me, and while my father did alienate the elites he was always loved by the commoners. Still, that is not enough, I need the Romans.
Smbat:
You know they threw me into the Hippodrome to be eaten by wild animals.
Khosrow:
I know they did. And I know the Byzantine empress loved you enough to beg for your life.
Smbat:
After I defeated a bear, a bull, and a lion, and the crowd turned in my favor.
Khosrow:
Which is why I am sending you back to Byzantium to recruit Maurice to my cause.
Smbat:
They impressed me into their infantry and sent me to fight in Africa.
Khosrow:
And did I not rescue you from that slaver market in Aleppo when you returned? We have too much history together to quibble about these small things. Go to Maurice. Offer him anything he wants. If he brings 40,000 men by spring, I would marry his daughter. If he brings 60,000 men by spring, I might enjoy a quick bath and could admit their one true God into the broadness of my heart.
Smbat:
And how am I supposed to travel to Byzantium?
Khosrow:
We will need Narses to accompany you there, to help make our case, and to lead the troops back.
Smbat:
You know that Narses has hated me ever since I betrayed him in the field of Martyropolis, switched sides and joined the Persian attack, so that your father could achieve the victory he so dearly craved.
Khosrow:
I know you did, and Eranshahr is grateful. But that was a long time ago. Narses is Armenian, you're Armenian, the time for petty grievances is past. You can work it out.
Smbat:
And for this task, should I be successful; what is my reward?
Khosrow:
Hereditary marzban of Armenia. My undying affection. Anything you want? Maurice will want the Kurdish towns, the Georgian ports, Syria, maybe even Nisibis. You can have Armenia, Dvin, you may deal with the Mamikonians as you wish, although we will need their helmets and their spears. Derbent and The Gates of the Caspian? All trans-Caucasian trade can be yours—they will want to pay you in salt, but you could take your pick of their goods in exchange for safe passage on the rest. Smbat, you are my brother, we may not be the same blood, but we are the same heart, and if you help me now you will live in my story forever. Consider it, think about it, make a free choice, but tonight, let's go see if there are maidens still awaiting us at the falls of Vahagn.

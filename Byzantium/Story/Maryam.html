Maryam

Context

Setting: Kashgar, a Silk Road village nestled in the crotch of the Pamirs, the Hindu Kush, and the Taklamakan desert. Maryam and her son Rostam are in a small bedroom, snuggling under fur blankets and speaking together, quietly, in Byzantine Greek.

Scene: A Mother's Tale

Rostam:
Mama, how did we get here?
Maryam:
We came over the mountains to the west. There was a Turkish herdsman to guide us and a yak for cartage, but I carried you on my back, and you cried the whole way. It was so cold, so cold, but having come over the pass I knew that our enemies could not follow us and that we would be safe here.
Rostam:
No mama, from the beginning. When I was a little girl…
Maryam:
That was a long time ago, and a long way from here. Aren't you tired?
Rostam:
From the beginning. When I was a little girl...
Maryam:
When I was a little girl I lived on an island in the middle of the sea. We called it Dēmónēsoi. So much water – from the narrow end, to the east, there was nothing but water, as far as you could see. The sea is salty, like Issykul, but not bitter, almost sweet. Waves lapped at the shore and the smell of the air was soft and alive. From the wide end, to the west, you could tell that we were not so far from land. There were hills, not like the mountains here, but steeply rising out of the water, and covered in green trees. And to the north there were boats and sails, and in the distance, the graceful dome of the Hagia Sophia and the city of Byzantium. My favorite thing was to go to the beach, collect shells and stones and arrange them in the sand, and imagine what my life could become.
Rostam:
Were you alone? Where was your mama?
Maryam:
I don't remember my mama. The closest memory I have is a feeling of love, unconditional love, but I can't see her face, I just know that she was there. I wasn't alone growing up, there were many other children, most around my age. It was kind of an orphanage for unwanted children of the court, not so dangerous that we needed to be killed, but not so innocent that we could be free. There was a dormitory where we slept, with beds and a fireplace, a cookhouse where we ate our meals, and a church for mass and instruction. There were nuns to teach us and priests to beat us; cooks to feed us and shepherds to make sure that the goats and the sheep that we slept with did not wander off a cliff and fall into the water.
Rostam:
We are not on an island. How did you leave the island?
Maryam:
We weren't always on the island. On special occasions: Easter, Christmas, someone's birthday, a boat would come and take us to the city, and we would be escorted to the cathedral for mass, to the Hippodrome for chariot races, or perhaps to the palace for a feast. Once at the Hippodrome there was an Armenian slave who fought a lion and survived – the crowd all shouted for him as he stood over the dead lion and gestured towards the royal box with his upraised fists. But at the end of it we were always taken back to the island. When I left the island for good I was seventeen? A boat came from the city, but I was the only one taken aboard. They brought me to the city, to a room high up in the palace, with candlesticks and a fire, and a lady of the court came to my room and told me that I had been selected, above all other girls, for a special mission, to serve God and the emperor and the country which had birthed me and owned me and kept me alive. I was to go to the east, to begin a life of the utmost importance, to marry a king.
Rostam:
Was that my papa?
Maryam:
Varkhuman is a good man, and you must honor him as sons honor fathers. To name your true father, to claim your birthright, aloud, is too dangerous, even here, beyond the mountains and the desert, far from Ctesiphon and where you were born. Look where Orion is in the sky. It is very late my love, aren't you tired?
Rostam:
No mama. How did you meet my papa? From the beginning, when I was a girl…
Maryam:
I wasn't a girl. A woman, I guess? I didn't really know what that meant but I had outgrown my childish shape. They kept me in the castle for months, and each day they would teach me things: the ways of a princess; how to dress in beautiful clothes, to adorn myself with creams and powders and combs and emollients; how to speak to a priest in the language of the church, to kneel and to pray, to cast my eyes down and to look people in the eye; to know what was important to the empire and what was important to a man and how to get my way.
Maryam:
Once a week a horrible old man, Procopius, would come to me. I was quite afraid of him, although he never touched me or beat me. He would always start with the sins of my grandmother Theodora; how she ruined an emperor, how she ruined an empire, how she lied and cheated and turned on her friends, how she performed the most despicable acts, how her soul was depraved and how the sins that she committed were so heinous that even two generations later it was still a blot on my soul that I must expiate by going to Persia, by keeping the faith, by carrying out any instructions from the emperor and by bearing and raising a Christian heir to the Sassanian throne.
Maryam:
So many things they tried to teach me about how to woo Khosrow and how to conquer Eranshahr but not a word of Persian.
Rostam:
But how did you talk?
Maryam:
(laughing) With a man and a woman there are things that you don't need words to say. But I learned. It's not like it is for you – you can meet someone from a different tribe and within an hour you are speaking their language as if it was your birthright. That is your gift, and it is a very special and rare gift indeed. For me it takes time, but I had time.
Maryam:
The journey east from Byzantium to Ani took many weeks. There was an Aramaic monk named Nestor who made me recite prayers in different languages: "I believe in one God, the father almighty", "Come to my aid, oh Mazda", "Our father, who art in heaven", "The will of the Lord is the law of righteousness". Not so useful in day-to-day conversation but in the right situation it could save your life. If I was on horseback Narses, a beardless man who had been in the military, would ride next to me and would teach me a few words in between long boring war stories about when he was young and had gone out into the field with the Byzantine army against Persians, Gokturks, and various other sects of blasphemers.
Maryam:
When we got to Ani I didn't see him at first. Smbat, an Armenian warrior, came to see me first. Narses translated for him into broken Greek as he described Khosrow: his qualities, his virtues, his manly beauty, and his royal mien. I didn't understand very much, there aren't many military words for what he wanted to say. Smbat sang me a song in Arabic, which was beautiful but which I did not understand until much later.

The Arabic Poem

(In Arabic)

"[She is] of medium height, with a clear skin color and fine teeth, white, gleaming like the moon, pronounced eyebrows, dark and wide eyed, with a high, aquiline nose, slender eyelashes over fine eyes, smooth cheeks, with a delectable body, plentiful hair, a good-sized skull so that ear-drops hang far apart, with a high neck, a wide bosom, and well-rounded breasts.

[She has] stout shoulder and upper arm bones, fine wrists, delicate hands with long and straight fingers; a pulled-in abdomen, neat waist, slender at the girdle, ample hips, a well-rounded rear and strong thighs, a fine posterior and fleshy buttocks, good-sized knees, filled-out calves so that her ornamental anklets fit snugly, but with delicate ankle bones and feet.

[She] walks with slow steps, is somnolent and remains inside in the fierce light of day and has a tender skin where this is exposed.

[She is] obedient to her lord and master, not flat-nosed or with a tanned skin, humble and submissive although of noble birth and not brought up in penurious circumstances, modest, sedate, mild in character, and steady minded.

[She has] noble maternal relatives and she is satisfied with her paternal lineage, without reference to her clan, and with her clan without reference to her tribe. Experience of life has made her of fine conduct and attainments (adab). Her ways of thought are those of noble people, but her actions those of the poor and needy.

[She is] skillful with her hands, restrained with her tongue, has a gentle voice; she is an adornment to the house, and puts the enemy to shame. If you wish for [sexual contact with] her, she shows eagerness for it; if you prefer to leave her alone, she is content to abstain. She becomes wide eyed [with sexual longing], her cheeks blush red, her lips tremble, and she hastens toward you before you can fall upon her."

Maryam:
Eventually Smbat saw my confusion, and instead of speaking he began to draw, and the delicacy of his art made me dream of the face in his picture each night when I went to sleep.
Maryam:
There were masses to attend and embassies to receive; there were many armies camped in the plains around the city, fresh recruits and supplies would arrive in dribs and drabs from the road we had taken as well as every other direction, diplomats and generals would shuttle between the camps, visiting cook fires and counting spears. One day Narses came to my tent and brought me to the central square; they put a veil of purple silk over my hair and seated me on a throne, and I sat quietly during a large ceremony in which ambassadors declaimed and warriors put down their weapons and priests performed rituals. When the sun finally set all the men in the square gave a great shout, cup bearers began distributing rough Georgian wine from Qvevri clay jars, and we feasted into the night.
Maryam:
The next day Narses came with an armed escort and took me into the hills above the city and met the Persian royal party. I didn't know him, but I knew who he was – he was tall and handsome and regal and well dressed; Persians obeyed his tiniest gesture and Romans slunk back into a defensive shell. He made everyone stay behind and motioned for me to walk with him. We went farther up the hill, out of sight of our guards, and he motioned for me to sit down on the ground, and from a pack he set out a lunch of dates, bread, cheese, and pomegranate. After we ate, he took my hand and pointed to the armies, the plain, the river, the hills, and everything out to the horizon, making a gesture towards his heart as if to say, "all this is mine". Then he reached over and made a gesture towards my heart as if to say, "all this is yours". I took the veil from my hair, looked into his face, and with my eyes I said "yes, yes, yes, yes".
Maryam:
Your father was Khosrow Parviz: king of kings, from a long line of kings, beloved by his people, feared by his enemies, and killed by his own son.
Rostam:
No Mama, too soon. From the beginning, when I was a young woman.
Maryam:
When I was a young woman I knew nothing of life. Now that I have lived I am a tiny bit wiser and much more tired. Aren't you sleepy?
Rostam:
Scheherazade told stories every night and her stories always last until dawn.
Maryam:
Pestilential child. I will sing you one song, and then you will go to sleep, and I will go to sleep, and perhaps tomorrow I will tell you an even more amazing and entertaining story. Deal?
Rostam:
Yes Mama. I love you.

Rostam nestles into the blankets and Maryam sings a lullaby. After she finishes the last note in the song, Maryam looks down at her lap where Rostam, lips open, is quietly snoring. She puts him down gently on the bedding, covers him up, puts out the fire, and prepares to leave the room. At the doorway she looks back and says "Sleep well my prince. Perhaps someday you will have to answer the prompt 'When I was a boy…'"

Rostam:
Mama, how did we get here?
Maryam:
From the beginning, when I was a little girl?
Rostam:
No Mama, that is too long and I always fall asleep. From the beginning, when I was a young woman.
Maryam:
Are you sure you don't want to hear the story of Kalia and Dimna? They are two jackals who serve a lion, who is king of the forest. Once upon a time …
Rostam:
No Mama. Our story. From the beginning, when I was a young woman, before you were born.
Maryam:
With the greatest pleasure, my prince. When I was a young bride I lived among many people and was also quite lonely.
